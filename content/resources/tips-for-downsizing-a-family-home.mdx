Downsizing a family home is one of those things that sounds straightforward until you're standing in the middle of it. Whether a parent is moving to a smaller place, transitioning to assisted living, or your family is settling an estate, the process touches on logistics and emotions in equal measure.

Here are some things we've learned from helping hundreds of families through it.

## Give Yourself More Time Than You Think You Need

Downsizing a home that's been lived in for decades doesn't happen in a weekend. Between sorting through belongings, making decisions about what to keep, coordinating with family members, and handling the sale or donation of everything else — it adds up fast.

If possible, start the process early. Even a few extra weeks makes a real difference in how manageable everything feels.

## Let Each Family Member Claim What Matters to Them

Before anything is sold, donated, or discarded, give every family member a chance to walk through the home and identify items they'd like to keep. This avoids conflict later and makes sure meaningful pieces stay in the family.

A few things that help:
- **Set a deadline** so the process doesn't drag on indefinitely.
- **Be specific about logistics** — If someone wants a piece of furniture, they need to arrange pickup by a certain date.
- **Respect each other's attachments** — What seems like clutter to one person may carry real meaning for another.

## Separate the Practical from the Sentimental

Not everything can come with you, and that's okay. It helps to think about belongings in a few categories:

- **Keep** — Things you use, need, or truly love.
- **Pass along** — Items a family member or friend would appreciate.
- **Sell** — Anything with resale value that you're ready to part with.
- **Donate** — Usable items that aren't worth selling individually.
- **Discard** — Things that are broken, worn out, or unsalvageable.

Working through the home one room at a time keeps it from becoming overwhelming.

## Take Photos Before You Let Go

If an item carries memories but not practical use — your mom's teacup collection, your dad's workbench setup — take a photo before it goes. It's a simple way to hold onto the memory without holding onto the object.

## Know That You Don't Have to Do This Alone

Sorting through a lifetime of belongings is physically and emotionally exhausting. There's no reason to white-knuckle it by yourself. Professional estate sale companies, senior move managers, and even just a trusted friend can make the process significantly easier.
